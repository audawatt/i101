Born in Birmingham, Alabama April 23, 1933, Annie J. Easley was highly encouraged by her mother to pursue a good education, taking a stand against racial laws. She graduated as valedictorian at her high school, and pursued college at Xavier University studying pharmacy. Work came easy to her, making her a very successful student in all her college level courses.

After moving to Cleveland to get closer to family, Easley found a new job at the National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics (NACA). She was a mathematician and computer engineer at NACA’s Lewis Flight Propulsion Laboratory, where she computed complex math functions manually.

NASA soon began to replace the “human computer” jobs with machines, so Easley decided to teach herself computer programming languages to stay ahead of the curve. After graduating college, in which she transferred to Cleveland State University to finish, she joined NASA’s Launch Vehicles Division to continue her computer code work.

Her most notable work at NASA includes her work on the Centaur Rocket, role as an equal employment opportunity counselor, and even contributing to projects that would lay the groundwork for hybrid vehicles that are in high demand today. Easley died June 25, 2011, in Cleveland, Ohio from natural causes. In 2021, ten years after her death, NASA named a small lunar crater after her.



Annie Easley was a computer scientist during the 1960s, an era when discrimination was rampant in all aspects of society. As a Black woman, Easley was especially prone to the prejudices of her time.

She grew up in a world designed to hold back people like her, through methods such as segregation and barriers to employment.

Even when she was hired by NASA, she still faced heavy racism, such as refusal of funds for basic training that all other NASA employees received, and even being cut out of promotional pictures.

Despite this, she overcame her challenges and even pioneered the way for fellow ethnic minorities to work in science and technology.



Annie Easley played a critical role in the field of technology during her time. She had worked at NASA and worked on alternative power technology.

This work developed the basis for the creation of hybrid vehicles and her work was directly used in the Centaur upper stage rocket. The process of early coding was a laborious one and to be able to code in this and code well is a mark of her intelligence and her skills in computational thinking and problem solving.

Her inventions are now something that will shape our future as more people turn to hybrid vehicles to reduce greenhouse gas emissions. Her work in climate change and the environment also extends to her research into damage to the ozone layer during the 70’s.

Later, she also worked on cleaner energy, such as solar power and power-plants that keep and reuse useful byproducts instead of discarding. Without her contributions, it may have taken years longer to develop the technology needed for these vehicles and in the process improved living conditions for all of those on earth



Easley not only played a part in laying the groundwork for the new age of vehicles but on top of this also worked to ensure that anyone could contribute to the technology, by working as an equal opportunity employer.

Easley drove great innovations in hybrid vehicle technology, the value of which can clearly be seen today. She broke barriers to education and employment for both people of color and women and helped advanced race and gender equality for all.

Hybrid cars are our future, and soon we hope all vehicles will switch to this way to help our planet’s environment stay stable. We can also guess that her work in equal employment paved the way for an uncountable number of other groups of people to be involved in technology, which will bring about great change and lead us to a better future.



We chose Annie Easley due to her impressive background and work in a variety of fields. Her work is still very relevant today and affects hundreds of people around the world. Not only was she a person with many groundbreaking accomplishments, but she also strove for equality.

Easley helped with outreach to youth in minority communities about STEM opportunities and advocated for equal employment practices. She was truly a woman ahead of her time, and was unafraid to let society tell her what she could and could not do.

Despite all of her success, she is still widely known, so it is important that we celebrate her work and not let her contributions go unrenowned.
